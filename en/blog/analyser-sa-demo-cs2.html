Method

How to analyze your CS2 demo like a pro (without spending 3 hours)

Reviewing a demo is long and you often watch the wrong rounds. Here is the 30-min method that surfaces the 3 mistakes that will cost you the next match.

April 22, 2026 8 min read By FragValue

Why 90% of self-reviews are useless

You just lost a match, you load the demo, you watch it for 2 hours, you say "yeah I could have done better" and you close it. Result: zero change, next match you make the same mistakes.

The problem is in the method, not the willpower. Watching an entire demo in free POV with no precise question is like reading a book without understanding the question you're trying to answer. You end up saturated.

The pro method does the opposite: questions first, demo second, focus on 5 to 6 rounds maximum, and always a written conclusion.

The 30-min rule

A productive analysis session lasts 30 min max. Beyond that, your brain saturates, you retain nothing, you become less critical. Better to do 30 min on a target match than 2 hours on a random demo.

The 3 questions to ask before you load the demo

Before you open CS2, take 2 minutes to write (not in your head, on paper or in Notes) these 3 questions. Without them, you will watch with no filter.

Question 1: which rounds was I useless in?

Look at the FACEIT scorecard for the match. Identify the rounds where you got 0 kill + 0 assist + you died early. These are your "ghost rounds": rounds where your team played 4v5.

Out of 22 rounds, 2 to 3 ghost rounds is normal. 5+ ghost rounds = serious positioning or decision problem.

Note the round numbers. You will jump straight to those rounds in the demo.

Question 2: which rounds did I lose while having the advantage?

Rounds lost in 2v5 are not interesting (too little info). The real learning rounds are the ones where:

  • You had a 4v3 numbers advantage and lost
  • You had a post-plant with utility and got retaken
  • You had the first kill and died on the next trade

These rounds reveal decisions consciously made under pressure that failed. That is where learning is maximal.

Question 3: what was my biggest economy loss?

Look at the economy graph. Identify the round where you go from full buy to full eco. Often it is round 3 or 4 (after a force loss), round 8 or 9 (after pistol loss), or round 14 or 15 (half anti-eco fail).

The question there is: which decision at round N-1 or N wrecked our economy? Save force? Over-commit on a hero play?

The round-by-round analysis grid (10 min)

Now that you have identified 3 to 5 target rounds, open the demo and for each round, fill this grid:

5 minutes per round max. If you stall on a round, move to the next. The point is not to be exhaustive but to surface patterns.

The questions to ask on each round

  1. Position: where should I have been at round start? Was I there?
  2. Info: what info did I have at 0:30? Was it correct?
  3. Decision: what decision did I make at the first enemy info? Why?
  4. Execution: were my aim and crosshair placement correct?
  5. Trade: if dead, did I get traded? If not, why?
Common trap

Don't focus only on lost duels. A round where you got 2 kills but should have made your teammate tradable, and he died = it is a bad round even with good stats. Impact > individual frags.

The 5 most common anti-patterns (15 min of targeted review)

Instead of watching everything, look specifically for these 5 patterns. 90% of mistakes at level 4-9 fall into one of them.

1. Over-peeking after a kill

Symptom: you kill, you keep the momentum, you push without utility, you get instantly traded.

What to look for: after each kill, do you fall back to cover? Do you check the mini-map before pushing on? Your default reflex should be "kill + retreat".

2. No utility ready

Symptom: you peek an angle without flash or smoke, you get cleaned.

What to look for: count how many grenades you have at the moment of the peek. If it is 0, you are at a structural disadvantage. You must always have at least 1 utility ready before a critical peek.

3. Bad rotate timing

Symptom: you rotate to the bombed site too late, you arrive when bomb is planted and you are alone.

What to look for: listen for the first enemy info noise. Calculate how long it takes you to arrive. Often people rotate at 30s left when they need 15s. They arrive, but exhausted and out of position.

4. Crosshair placement at floor level

Symptom: you get headshot on angles you "could have" seen.

What to look for: pause every time you advance through a hallway. Look at your crosshair. Is it at head level? 70% of the time, level 4-7 players have their crosshair too low (they flick) or too high. The fix takes 2 weeks of targeted deathmatch.

5. Abandoning your zone without a call

Symptom: your CT site is stack 2 but you decide to rotate alone on "push other" info.

What to look for: did you abandon your spot before confirmed info, or without your IGL saying "rotate"? Simple rule: on CT, you move only on solid info or IGL call. Never on "I think".

Conclusion: the written summary (5 min)

Close the demo. Write 3 bullets:

  • Pattern 1: my most repeated mistake today (e.g., "I push without utility 4 times out of 22 rounds")
  • Pattern 2: a decision that cost me dearly (e.g., "save force round 9 instead of full eco")
  • Fix for next match: one concrete thing I will correct (e.g., "I never peek without flash or smoke pop")

One single fix per match. If you try to correct 3 things at once, you correct zero.

Write it down somewhere

A physical notebook or a Notion doc dedicated to your reviews. The act of writing forces your brain to structure and retain. You will reread your summaries after 3 weeks and laugh at how the same patterns repeat, then watch them disappear.

The pro demos trap

"I'll watch a pro play Mirage to learn." Not really.

Pros play with 4 teammates following a playbook. Their decisions make sense in THAT context. If you copy s1mple at level 5 with 4 solo-queue teammates, you will die.

Good use of pro demos:

  • Watch utility lineups (smokes, molotovs): copy them directly
  • Watch crosshair placement on critical peeks
  • Watch execute timings: when they call info, when they commit

What does NOT copy directly: aggressive plays, positioning decisions (depend on your teammates), IGL calls.
