Progression

From Level 4 to Level 10 FACEIT: a concrete tier-by-tier roadmap

Each level has its required skills. Level 4-6: the fundamentals. Level 6-8: adaptation. Level 8-10: coordination. We detail what to work on at each tier, with minimum target stats to hit.

April 22, 2026 8 min read By FragValue

The truth about FACEIT levels

A lot of people treat the FACEIT level as an abstract number. "I'm level 7." As if it were a fixed rank. In reality, each tier represents a skill ceiling, and you only break through it once you add the missing skills.

The classic problem: a level 5 who farms 500 hours but stays level 5. His aim is OK, but he is missing the game sense to progress. He can tap 20 kills per match and still be level 5 because he loses the key rounds.

What the FACEIT level actually measures

The FACEIT level is based on your ELO, which moves with: your wins and losses, the skill of your opponents, and the round difference. You gain more ELO beating someone stronger and less beating someone weaker. The level is a visual translation of your ELO.

Level 4: 801-950 ELO / Level 5: 951-1100 / Level 6: 1101-1250 / Level 7: 1251-1400 / Level 8: 1401-1550 / Level 9: 1551-1700 / Level 10: 1701+

Tier 1: Level 4 to 6, the fundamentals

The biggest gap for many players. People often stay here for 300 to 500 hours if they don't work on the right things.

What makes the difference at this tier

  • Crosshair placement: you keep your crosshair at head level at all times
  • AK and M4 spray control: you can dump 15 bullets without a chaotic spray reset
  • Basic CT positioning: you hold your angle, you don't push without info
  • Map knowledge: you know 2 to 3 maps by heart (callouts, basic smokes)
  • Economy basics: you know when to force and when to save

4 to 6 week action plan

  1. Week 1-2: 20 min of aim lab per day (scenarios Gridshot, 1wall6targets) + 2 FACEIT matches. Focus: crosshair placement and HS%.
  2. Week 3-4: add a 15-min FACEIT deathmatch before each session. Focus: spray control and reflexes on AK and M4.
  3. Week 5-6: learn 4 smokes and 4 key molotovs on Mirage and Dust2 (your 2 most-played maps). Practice in community servers.
Classic level 4-6 trap

Changing sensitivity or crosshair every 2 weeks because "it doesn't feel right". Lock your setup for at least 3 months. Otherwise you blame your progress or regression on your setup instead of your decisions.

Tier 2: Level 6 to 8, adaptation

This is where you understand that CS2 is a game of information more than a game of reflexes. Your aim doesn't change much, but your decisions need to get smarter.

What makes the difference

  • Utility usage: you use your grenades before critical duels
  • Trade fragging: you are never alone peeking, you always have a teammate backing
  • Rotate timing: you rotate at the right moment, not too early not too late
  • Advanced economy: you can read your opponent's buys, you force and eco optimally
  • Map pool: you have 4 to 5 solid maps, you can play different CT and T sides

6 to 8 week action plan

  1. Week 1-2: add 30 min per week of advanced utility lineups (learn one-way smokes, anti-peek molotovs, entry flashes per map).
  2. Week 3-4: review 1 demo per week with the 30-min method. Focus: rounds you lost while having the advantage.
  3. Week 5-6: find a stable duo. Play 70% of your matches with that person. 2-player coordination beats 5-player coordination at the start.
  4. Week 7-8: add 2 new maps to your rotation (go from 3 to 5 mastered maps). Practice mode the basic smokes plus 1 match per new map per week.

Tier 3: Level 8 to 10, coordination

The tier where individual play is no longer enough. Level 8-10 players all have a solid aim. What separates level 10 from level 8 is coordination with teammates, even in solo queue.

What makes the difference

  • Effective comms: you give the right info, at the right moment, without polluting the channel
  • Adapting to your team: you adjust your role depending on who IGLs and who AWPs
  • Reading the enemy team: you anticipate the opposing team's patterns after 5 rounds
  • Consistency: very few matches below 0.80 FV Rating, even your "off days" stay decent
  • Clutch factor: you win 25%+ of your 1v1s, 15%+ of your 1v2s

2 to 3 month action plan

  1. Month 1: find a fixed team (5-stacks) or a clan that plays regularly. Even 2 coordinated 5v5 matches per week move you forward more than 20 solo queue games.
  2. Month 2: play amateur tournaments (ESEA, Challengermode, FACEIT Open cash cups). The pressure level is different. You discover your weaknesses under stress.
  3. Month 3: work on consistency more than peak performance. Goal: 0 matches below 0.80 FV Rating across 20 games. When you eliminate your "off day" matches, you cross the ELO threshold.
The secret of stable level 10s

They manage their mental and their fatigue. They don't queue past 11pm if they work the next day. They stop after 2 losses. They take a 10-min break between matches. They eat and sleep properly. That is the difference between "I am capable of being level 10" and "I am stable at level 10".

Realistic timings

With serious, structured work (1 to 2 hours per day of focused CS2, not 4 hours of farming):

  • Level 4 to 6: 2 to 4 months
  • Level 6 to 8: 4 to 8 months
  • Level 8 to 10: 6 to 12 months

Total to go from 4 to 10: 12 to 24 months depending on your starting point, available time and discipline. It can be faster if you come from CSGO with solid fundamentals, longer if you are a beginner.

What not to do

Smurfing in low ELO solo queue to "feel better". You learn nothing. Worse, you build habits that no longer work at your real level.

Boycotting demo review because "it's boring". This is THE single most profitable progress multiplier.

Rage-queueing after a loss. Your decisions are wrecked, your aim is timid, you fuel your own spiral.
